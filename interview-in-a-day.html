01System Design Interview Framework

45-MIN INTERVIEW STRUCTURE
MIN 0–5
Clarify Requirements — You Drive This

Ask before designing. "Who are the users? What scale — DAU, requests/sec? Read vs write ratio? Latency SLA? Consistency requirements? Global or single region?" Interviewers reward engineers who discover requirements, not wait for them.

MIN 5–10
Capacity Estimation + Numbers

Back-of-envelope math shows engineering judgment. "100M users, 10% DAU = 10M/day. 1 post each = 115 writes/sec. 20x read:write = 2300 reads/sec. 1KB/post × 10M = 10GB/day." Order of magnitude is the point — round aggressively.

MIN 10–20
High-Level Design — APIs + Core Flow

Define the APIs first: what does the client call? Then draw the main flow: client → load balancer → service → cache → DB. Stay on one level of abstraction. Explicitly state what you're skipping ("I'll skip auth, focusing on the write path").

MIN 20–35
Deep Dive — Pick the Hardest Component

Ask "Where should I go deep?" or pick it yourself. Show internals: why Cassandra for messages (write-optimized LSM), why Redis ZSET for feeds (sorted by score, O(log n) insert), why Kafka for fan-out (durable, replay, backpressure handling).

MIN 35–45
Trade-offs + Bottlenecks + Wrap-Up

Proactively raise issues before they're asked. "One bottleneck here is celebrity fan-out — I'd handle this with hybrid push/pull." Discuss trade-offs. End with: "What aspect would you like me to go deeper on?"

Latency Numbers to Know

  • L1 cache hit: ~1ns · L2: ~4ns · RAM: ~100ns
  • SSD read: ~16μs · HDD seek: ~10ms
  • Network same datacenter: ~500μs
  • Network cross-region: ~150ms
  • Read 1MB from RAM: ~250μs · SSD: ~1ms
  • 1M req/day ≈ 12 req/sec · 1B req/day ≈ 11.5K/sec

Pick the Right Database

  • ACID transactions, complex joins → PostgreSQL
  • Write-heavy, 100K+ writes/sec → Cassandra
  • Flexible schema, JSON nesting → MongoDB
  • Cache, session, leaderboard, pub-sub → Redis
  • Graph traversal (social, fraud) → Neo4j
  • Object storage (files, images, video) → S3 + CDN

Top 8 Design Questions

  • WhatsApp / Slack — real-time messaging
  • YouTube / Netflix — video at scale
  • Twitter / Instagram — feed & fan-out
  • Uber / Lyft — real-time geo matching
  • Google Drive — file sync & storage
  • Rate Limiter — distributed token bucket
  • Notification System — multi-channel delivery
  • URL Shortener — hash + redirect at scale

CAP Theorem in Practice

  • P (partition) always happens — choose C or A
  • CP: strong consistency, less availability (payments, banking)
  • AP: high availability, eventual consistency (social feeds, DNS)
  • PACELC: even without partition, latency vs consistency
  • Redis: AP by default (async replication)
  • PostgreSQL sync replication: CP

02Behavioral Interviews — STAR Method

The STAR Framework

Situation — Context in 1-2 sentences. Project, team size, stakes.
Task — Your specific responsibility. Distinguish "I" from "the team".
Action — 70% of your answer. What did YOU specifically do, decide, build? Name the technical choices and why.
Result — Always quantify. "P99 latency from 250ms to 18ms", "60% reduction in deployment time", "zero incidents in 6 months". Vague results signal weak answers.

Q: "Tell me about a difficult technical decision you made under uncertainty."
Situation

Production DB couldn't handle projected write load for an upcoming launch in 3 weeks. Risk of customer-facing downtime.

Task

I was lead engineer responsible for choosing the storage solution and migrating 50M rows without downtime.

Action

Compared Cassandra vs sharded PostgreSQL vs DynamoDB. Chose Cassandra for LSM-tree write performance. Built dual-write migration: new writes to both stores, backfilled old data, shifted read traffic 10% at a time, monitored error rates at each step with Grafana dashboards.

Result

Zero-downtime migration in 10 days. Write throughput from 5K to 80K/sec. Feature launched on time, no production incidents.

Q: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical direction."
Situation

Team planning to build a custom message queue to save infra cost, despite unclear requirements and 3-month timeline.

Task

I was scoping the implementation but believed we were solving the wrong problem.

Action

Prepared a data-driven TCO comparison: custom build (2 engineers × 6 months) vs managed Kafka ($800/month). Presented to tech lead with risk analysis. Proposed Kafka with a 2-week integration spike instead. Used data, not opinion.

Result

Team adopted Kafka. Saved ~4 months of engineering. I led the integration. System has run reliably for 18 months with zero major incidents.

Q: "Describe a time you improved a system's reliability or performance."
Situation

Service hitting 8% error rate under load. On-call alerts every other night. Customer complaints escalating.

Task

I was the primary owner of this service and responsible for diagnosing and fixing it.

Action

Used distributed tracing (Jaeger) to isolate the bottleneck — a DB query missing a composite index on a high-frequency path. Added index, implemented PgBouncer connection pooling, added circuit breakers on downstream dependencies.

Result

Error rate: 8% → 0.02%. P99 latency: 1.2s → 90ms. Zero on-call pages for 4 months post-fix.

03Coding Interview Strategy

PROBLEM-SOLVING FRAMEWORK (45 MIN)
MIN 0–3
Understand + Clarify

Read aloud. Ask: "Can the array be empty? Duplicates allowed? Input size?" Confirm examples. Never assume constraints.

MIN 3–8
Think Aloud — Brute Force First

State the naive approach first. "The brute force is O(n²) — nested loops. It works but won't scale. Let me optimize." Interviewers want your thinking, not just code.

MIN 8–15
Optimize — Pattern Recognition

What pattern fits? Sliding window for subarrays, two pointers for sorted arrays, hash map for O(1) lookup, stack for nested structures, BFS for shortest path, DP for overlapping subproblems.

MIN 15–35
Code — Clean + Narrated

Meaningful variable names. Comment the key insight. Handle edge cases as you go and say so aloud. "I'm checking the empty case here." Don't code silently.

MIN 35–45
Test + Complexity

Trace your example by hand. Then test one edge case. Finally: "This is O(n) time — one pass. O(n) space — hash map stores at most n elements."

Pattern → When to Use

  • Sliding Window — max/min subarray of size k, substrings
  • Two Pointers — sorted array, pair sum, palindrome check
  • Hash Map — frequency count, two-sum, O(1) lookup
  • Stack — matching brackets, next greater element
  • BFS — shortest path, level-order, unweighted graph
  • DFS — all paths, tree traversal, island count
  • Binary Search — sorted array, find first/last position
  • DP — overlapping subproblems: knapsack, LCS, edit distance

Complexity Reference

  • O(1) — HashMap get/set, array index access
  • O(log n) — binary search, balanced BST
  • O(n) — single pass, linear scan
  • O(n log n) — merge sort, heapsort, TreeMap ops
  • O(n²) — nested loops, bubble sort
  • O(2ⁿ) — all subsets, recursive Fibonacci
  • O(n!) — all permutations
  • Space O(n) — storing input-sized data structure

Edge Cases to Always Test

  • Empty input / null
  • Single element array
  • All duplicate values
  • Already sorted / reverse sorted
  • Negative numbers
  • Integer overflow (large sums)
  • Disconnected graph (multiple components)
  • Cycle in linked list or graph

Common Interview Traps

  • ❌ Jumping to design before clarifying requirements
  • ❌ "I'd use microservices" without justifying the split
  • ❌ Proposing a perfect system with no trade-offs
  • ❌ Behavioral answers with no quantified results
  • ❌ Coding in silence — always narrate your thinking
  • ❌ Waiting to be asked about trade-offs — raise them yourself

How to Answer "Explain X" Technical Questions

3-layer structure — always:

1. What: One sentence definition. "MVCC is a concurrency control mechanism that lets multiple readers and one writer operate without blocking each other."

2. How: Explain the mechanism. "Each write creates a new row version with a transaction timestamp. Readers see a snapshot at their transaction start time — old versions are kept until no active transaction needs them, then VACUUM cleans them up."

3. Trade-off / When: Show depth. "The downside is storage bloat from old row versions and the cost of VACUUM. You'd use MVCC in any OLTP system that needs high read throughput — PostgreSQL, MySQL InnoDB, CockroachDB all use it."

05Compensation Strategy

5 Rules for Every Negotiation

1. Never state your number first. "I'd prefer to understand the full scope and compensation structure first. Can you share the range?" If pressed: "I'm flexible and looking at the full picture — what's the band?"

2. Never accept on the spot. "Thank you — I'm very excited about this. Can I take 24 hours to review the full package?" Always. Even if you're certain.

3. Negotiate base and equity separately. Get the highest base. Then negotiate equity. Different budget pools — companies often have more flexibility on one.

4. Counter 20–30% above the offer. The worst they say is no. "I'm excited about the role. The offer is below my target — I was expecting X based on market data and the scope. Is there flexibility?" They almost always come back with something.

5. Use competing offers as leverage. Even a process elsewhere works. "I'm also in process with Y — I'd love to make this work, but need the comp to be competitive."

07Day-Before Checklist

24 Hours Out — Final Prep

✓ Have 3 STAR stories ready: one technical decision, one conflict/disagreement, one production incident you owned. Quantified results for each.
✓ Practice "Tell me about yourself" out loud — 90 seconds. Hit: current role + key technical impact + why this company specifically.
✓ Know your comp target. Base, equity separately. Your minimum acceptable number. A justification ready: "based on market data and the scope of the role."
✓ Read the company's recent engineering blog posts. Know 1-2 specific technical challenges they've solved — mention it during the interview to signal genuine interest.
✓ Have 3 smart questions ready for "Do you have any questions for us?" — about technical challenges, team culture, and growth paths.
✓ Review the latency numbers, DB selection framework, and your 3 STAR stories one last time. Then stop.
✓ Sleep 8 hours. Eat well. No last-minute cramming. Your best asset in the interview is clear thinking — not crammed trivia.
